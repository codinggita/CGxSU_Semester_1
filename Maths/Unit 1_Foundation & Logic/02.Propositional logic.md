# Propositional Logic

## Learning Objectives

- Define propositions rigorously and distinguish them from non-propositions
- Construct compound propositions using logical connectives (negation, conjunction, disjunction, implication, biconditional)
- Build and analyze truth tables for propositions of arbitrary complexity
- Identify and prove tautologies, contradictions, and contingencies
- Apply De Morgan's Laws and logical equivalences to simplify complex expressions
- Translate natural language statements into propositional logic and vice versa

## Prerequisites

- Basic understanding of true/false values
- Familiarity with set notation and membership
- Ability to work with tables and systematic enumeration

## Definitions and Theoretical Foundations

### Definition 3.1 (Proposition)
A **proposition** (or **statement**) is a declarative sentence that is either true or false, but not both.

**Examples of propositions:**
- "$2 + 2 = 4$" (true)
- "$5 > 7$" (false)
- "Paris is the capital of France" (true)

**Examples of non-propositions:**
- "Close the door" (command, no truth value)
- "What time is it?" (question, no truth value)
- "$x + 2 = 5$" (open sentence, truth depends on $x$)

**Convention:** Propositions are typically denoted by lowercase letters: $p, q, r, s, \ldots$

### Definition 3.2 (Truth Value)
The **truth value** of a proposition is either **True** (T or 1) or **False** (F or 0).

### Definition 3.3 (Logical Connectives)

Logical connectives are operators that combine or modify propositions to form compound propositions.

#### 1. Negation
The **negation** of a proposition $p$, denoted $\neg p$ (or $\sim p$ or $\overline{p}$), is the proposition "not $p$."

**Truth Table:**

| $p$ | $\neg p$ |
|-----|----------|
| T   | F        |
| F   | T        |

**Properties:**
- $\neg(\neg p) = p$ (double negation law)

#### 2. Conjunction
The **conjunction** of propositions $p$ and $q$, denoted $p \land q$ (read "$p$ and $q$"), is true if and only if both $p$ and $q$ are true.

**Truth Table:**

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \land q$ |
|-----|-----|-------------|
| T   | T   | T           |
| T   | F   | F           |
| F   | T   | F           |
| F   | F   | F           |

#### 3. Disjunction
The **disjunction** of propositions $p$ and $q$, denoted $p \lor q$ (read "$p$ or $q$"), is true if at least one of $p$ or $q$ is true.

**Truth Table:**

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \lor q$ |
|-----|-----|------------|
| T   | T   | T          |
| T   | F   | T          |
| F   | T   | T          |
| F   | F   | F          |

**Note:** This is **inclusive or**, meaning both can be true simultaneously.

#### 4. Implication (Conditional)
The **implication** (or **conditional**) from $p$ to $q$, denoted $p \to q$ (read "if $p$ then $q$"), is false only when $p$ is true and $q$ is false.

**Truth Table:**

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \to q$ |
|-----|-----|-----------|
| T   | T   | T         |
| T   | F   | F         |
| F   | T   | T         |
| F   | F   | T         |

**Terminology:**
- $p$ is the **hypothesis** (or **antecedent**)
- $q$ is the **conclusion** (or **consequent**)

**Equivalent form:** $p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q$

**Related forms:**
- **Converse:** $q \to p$
- **Inverse:** $\neg p \to \neg q$
- **Contrapositive:** $\neg q \to \neg p$ (logically equivalent to $p \to q$)

#### 5. Biconditional
The **biconditional** of $p$ and $q$, denoted $p \leftrightarrow q$ (read "$p$ if and only if $q$" or "$p$ iff $q$"), is true when $p$ and $q$ have the same truth value.

**Truth Table:**

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \leftrightarrow q$ |
|-----|-----|-----------------------|
| T   | T   | T                     |
| T   | F   | F                     |
| F   | T   | F                     |
| F   | F   | T                     |

**Equivalent form:** $p \leftrightarrow q \equiv (p \to q) \land (q \to p)$

### Definition 3.4 (Truth Table)
A **truth table** is a systematic enumeration of all possible truth value assignments to propositional variables and the resulting truth values of compound propositions.

**For $n$ variables, there are $2^n$ rows** (one for each possible combination of truth values).

### Definition 3.5 (Tautology, Contradiction, Contingency)

- A **tautology** is a proposition that is always true, regardless of the truth values of its components. Example: $p \lor \neg p$

- A **contradiction** is a proposition that is always false, regardless of the truth values of its components. Example: $p \land \neg p$

- A **contingency** is a proposition that is neither a tautology nor a contradiction (sometimes true, sometimes false).

### Definition 3.6 (Logical Equivalence)
Two propositions $P$ and $Q$ are **logically equivalent**, denoted $P \equiv Q$ or $P \Leftrightarrow Q$, if they have identical truth values for all possible truth value assignments.

Equivalently, $P \leftrightarrow Q$ is a tautology.

### Fundamental Logical Equivalences

**Identity Laws:**
- $p \land \text{T} \equiv p$
- $p \lor \text{F} \equiv p$

**Domination Laws:**
- $p \lor \text{T} \equiv \text{T}$
- $p \land \text{F} \equiv \text{F}$

**Idempotent Laws:**
- $p \lor p \equiv p$
- $p \land p \equiv p$

**Double Negation:**
- $\neg(\neg p) \equiv p$

**Commutative Laws:**
- $p \lor q \equiv q \lor p$
- $p \land q \equiv q \land p$

**Associative Laws:**
- $(p \lor q) \lor r \equiv p \lor (q \lor r)$
- $(p \land q) \land r \equiv p \land (q \land r)$

**Distributive Laws:**
- $p \lor (q \land r) \equiv (p \lor q) \land (p \lor r)$
- $p \land (q \lor r) \equiv (p \land q) \lor (p \land r)$

**De Morgan's Laws:**
- $\neg(p \land q) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q$
- $\neg(p \lor q) \equiv \neg p \land \neg q$

**Absorption Laws:**
- $p \lor (p \land q) \equiv p$
- $p \land (p \lor q) \equiv p$

**Negation Laws:**
- $p \lor \neg p \equiv \text{T}$
- $p \land \neg p \equiv \text{F}$

**Implication Equivalences:**
- $p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q$
- $p \to q \equiv \neg q \to \neg p$ (contrapositive)
- $\neg(p \to q) \equiv p \land \neg q$

**Biconditional Equivalences:**
- $p \leftrightarrow q \equiv (p \to q) \land (q \to p)$
- $p \leftrightarrow q \equiv (p \land q) \lor (\neg p \land \neg q)$

## Worked Examples

### Example 1 (Simple): Identifying Propositions

**Problem:** Determine which of the following are propositions:

(a) "$3 + 5 = 8$"

(b) "Is it raining?"

(c) "$x > 10$"

(d) "Read this chapter"

(e) "The square root of 16 is 4"

**Solution:**

(a) **Proposition** (declarative, true) ✓

(b) **Not a proposition** (question) ✗

(c) **Not a proposition** (open sentence, depends on value of $x$) ✗

(d) **Not a proposition** (command) ✗

(e) **Proposition** (declarative, true) ✓

---

### Example 2 (Intermediate): Constructing Truth Tables

**Problem:** Construct a truth table for $(p \lor q) \to (p \land q)$.

**Solution:**

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \lor q$ | $p \land q$ | $(p \lor q) \to (p \land q)$ |
|-----|-----|------------|-------------|------------------------------|
| T   | T   | T          | T           | T                            |
| T   | F   | T          | F           | F                            |
| F   | T   | T          | F           | F                            |
| F   | F   | F          | F           | T                            |

**Analysis:** The proposition is true when both $p$ and $q$ are true, or when both are false. It is a **contingency** (neither tautology nor contradiction).

---

### Example 3 (Intermediate): Proving a Tautology

**Problem:** Prove that $p \lor \neg p$ is a tautology.

**Solution:**

| $p$ | $\neg p$ | $p \lor \neg p$ |
|-----|----------|-----------------|
| T   | F        | T               |
| F   | T        | T               |

Since $p \lor \neg p$ is true for all possible truth values of $p$, it is a **tautology**. $\square$

This is called the **Law of Excluded Middle**.

---

### Example 4 (Challenging): Proving Logical Equivalence Using Truth Tables

**Problem:** Prove that $p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q$ using a truth table.

**Solution:**

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \to q$ | $\neg p$ | $\neg p \lor q$ |
|-----|-----|-----------|----------|-----------------|
| T   | T   | T         | F        | T               |
| T   | F   | F         | F        | F               |
| F   | T   | T         | T        | T               |
| F   | F   | T         | T        | T               |

Since columns for $p \to q$ and $\neg p \lor q$ are identical, we conclude:
$$p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q$$ $\square$

This equivalence is fundamental for transforming implications into disjunctions.

---

### Example 5 (Challenging): Applying De Morgan's Laws

**Problem:** Simplify $\neg((p \land q) \lor (\neg p \land r))$ using De Morgan's Laws.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Apply De Morgan's Law to the outer negation:
$$\neg((p \land q) \lor (\neg p \land r)) \equiv \neg(p \land q) \land \neg(\neg p \land r)$$

**Step 2:** Apply De Morgan's Law to each negated conjunction:
$$\neg(p \land q) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q$$
$$\neg(\neg p \land r) \equiv \neg(\neg p) \lor \neg r \equiv p \lor \neg r$$

**Step 3:** Combine:
$$(\neg p \lor \neg q) \land (p \lor \neg r)$$

**Final Answer:** $(\neg p \lor \neg q) \land (p \lor \neg r)$ $\square$

---

### Example 6 (Advanced): Proving Equivalence Algebraically

**Problem:** Prove that $(p \to q) \land (p \to r) \equiv p \to (q \land r)$ using logical equivalences (without truth tables).

**Solution:**

**LHS (Left-Hand Side):**
$$\begin{align}
(p \to q) \land (p \to r) &\equiv (\neg p \lor q) \land (\neg p \lor r) \quad \text{(implication equivalence)}\\
&\equiv \neg p \lor (q \land r) \quad \text{(distributive law)}\\
&\equiv p \to (q \land r) \quad \text{(implication equivalence)}
\end{align}$$

Therefore, $(p \to q) \land (p \to r) \equiv p \to (q \land r)$. $\square$

## Applications and Intuition

### Computer Science: Boolean Logic
Propositional logic directly corresponds to Boolean algebra in programming:
- $\land$ ↔ AND operator (`&&` in C/Java, `and` in Python)
- $\lor$ ↔ OR operator (`||` in C/Java, `or` in Python)
- $\neg$ ↔ NOT operator (`!` in C/Java, `not` in Python)

### Digital Logic Circuits
Logical connectives correspond to logic gates:
- AND gate implements $p \land q$
- OR gate implements $p \lor q$
- NOT gate implements $\neg p$
- Complex circuits implement compound propositions

### Conditional Statements in Programming
The implication $p \to q$ models if-then constructs:
```python
if p:  # if p is true
    q  # then q must execute
```

### Database Queries
SQL WHERE clauses use propositional logic:
```sql
SELECT * FROM users WHERE (age > 18) AND (status = 'active')
```
This implements $(p \land q)$ where $p$: age > 18, $q$: status = 'active'.

### Exercise 6 (Challenge)
Prove using logical equivalences (without truth tables): $\neg(p \leftrightarrow q) \equiv (p \land \neg q) \lor (\neg p \land q)$.

**Hint:** Expand $p \leftrightarrow q$ using its definition, then apply De Morgan's Laws.

**Solution:**

$$\begin{align}
\neg(p \leftrightarrow q) &\equiv \neg((p \to q) \land (q \to p)) \quad \text{(definition)}\\
&\equiv \neg((\neg p \lor q) \land (\neg q \lor p)) \quad \text{(implication equiv.)}\\
&\equiv \neg(\neg p \lor q) \lor \neg(\neg q \lor p) \quad \text{(De Morgan)}\\
&\equiv (p \land \neg q) \lor (q \land \neg p) \quad \text{(De Morgan, double neg.)}\\
&\equiv (p \land \neg q) \lor (\neg p \land q) \quad \text{(commutative)}
\end{align}$$

Therefore, $\neg(p \leftrightarrow q) \equiv (p \land \neg q) \lor (\neg p \land q)$. $\square$

---

### Exercise 7 (Challenge)
Show that the contrapositive $\neg q \to \neg p$ is logically equivalent to $p \to q$.

**Hint:** Use the equivalence $p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q$ twice.

**Solution:**

$$\begin{align}
\neg q \to \neg p &\equiv \neg(\neg q) \lor \neg p \quad \text{(implication equiv.)}\\
&\equiv q \lor \neg p \quad \text{(double negation)}\\
&\equiv \neg p \lor q \quad \text{(commutative)}\\
&\equiv p \to q \quad \text{(implication equiv.)}
\end{align}$$

Therefore, $\neg q \to \neg p \equiv p \to q$. $\square$

## Common Mistakes and Misconceptions

1. **Confusing implication with biconditional:** $p \to q$ does not mean $q \to p$. Only biconditional ($p \leftrightarrow q$) implies both directions.

2. **Misunderstanding implication truth values:** $p \to q$ is true when $p$ is false, regardless of $q$. This is counterintuitive but crucial.

3. **Incorrect application of De Morgan's Laws:** $\neg(p \land q) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q$ (AND becomes OR), not $\neg p \land \neg q$.

4. **Confusing OR with XOR:** Logical OR ($\lor$) is inclusive (both can be true). Exclusive OR (XOR) is true when exactly one is true.

5. **Negating implications incorrectly:** $\neg(p \to q) \equiv p \land \neg q$, not $\neg p \to \neg q$.

6. **Assuming converse equivalence:** $p \to q$ is NOT equivalent to its converse $q \to p$. They are independent.

## Summary and Key Takeaways

1. **Propositions** are declarative sentences with definite truth values (true or false).

2. **Logical connectives** (negation, conjunction, disjunction, implication, biconditional) combine propositions to form compound propositions.

3. **Truth tables** systematically enumerate all truth value possibilities and verify logical properties.

4. **Tautologies** are always true; **contradictions** are always false; **contingencies** vary.

5. **De Morgan's Laws** transform negations of conjunctions/disjunctions and are essential for logical simplification.

6. **Logical equivalences** enable algebraic manipulation of propositions without truth tables, streamlining complex proofs.

7. **Contrapositive equivalence** ($p \to q \equiv \neg q \to \neg p$) is a powerful proof technique.
