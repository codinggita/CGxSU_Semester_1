## Definitions and Theoretical Foundations

### Review: Logical Equivalence

**Definition 4.1 (Logical Equivalence):**
Two compound propositions $P$ and $Q$ are **logically equivalent**, denoted $P \equiv Q$, if $P \leftrightarrow Q$ is a tautology. Equivalently, $P$ and $Q$ have identical truth values for all possible truth value assignments to their propositional variables.

### Implication and Related Forms

**Definition 4.2 (Forms of Implication):**
Given an implication $p \to q$, we define:

1. **Converse:** $q \to p$
2. **Inverse:** $\neg p \to \neg q$
3. **Contrapositive:** $\neg q \to \neg p$

**Theorem 4.1 (Contrapositive Equivalence):**
An implication is logically equivalent to its contrapositive:
$$p \to q \equiv \neg q \to \neg p$$

**Proof:** Using truth tables or algebraic manipulation:
$$\begin{align}
p \to q &\equiv \neg p \lor q \quad \text{(implication equiv.)}\\
&\equiv q \lor \neg p \quad \text{(commutative)}\\
\neg q \to \neg p &\equiv \neg(\neg q) \lor \neg p \quad \text{(implication equiv.)}\\
&\equiv q \lor \neg p \quad \text{(double negation)}
\end{align}$$
Since both reduce to $q \lor \neg p$, they are equivalent. $\square$

**Important:** The **converse** and **inverse** are NOT equivalent to the original implication.

**Theorem 4.2:** The converse and inverse are logically equivalent to each other:
$$q \to p \equiv \neg p \to \neg q$$

### Predicate Logic

**Definition 4.3 (Predicate):**
A **predicate** is a statement containing one or more variables that becomes a proposition when specific values are substituted for the variables.

**Notation:** $P(x)$, $Q(x, y)$, $R(x, y, z)$, etc.

**Examples:**
- $P(x)$: "$x > 5$" (predicate with one variable)
- $Q(x, y)$: "$x + y = 10$" (predicate with two variables)
- $R(x)$: "$x$ is prime" (predicate)

**Domain (Universe of Discourse):** The set of all possible values that variables can take.

### Quantifiers

**Definition 4.4 (Universal Quantifier):**
The **universal quantifier** $\forall$ (read "for all") asserts that a predicate holds for all values in the domain.

$$\forall x \, P(x)$$
means "$P(x)$ is true for every $x$ in the domain."

**Definition 4.5 (Existential Quantifier):**
The **existential quantifier** $\exists$ (read "there exists") asserts that a predicate holds for at least one value in the domain.

$$\exists x \, P(x)$$
means "There exists at least one $x$ in the domain such that $P(x)$ is true."

**Definition 4.6 (Unique Existential Quantifier):**
The notation $\exists! x \, P(x)$ means "there exists exactly one $x$ such that $P(x)$ is true."

$$\exists! x \, P(x) \equiv \exists x \, (P(x) \land \forall y \, (P(y) \to y = x))$$

### Quantifier Negation Rules

**Theorem 4.3 (De Morgan's Laws for Quantifiers):**

1. $\neg(\forall x \, P(x)) \equiv \exists x \, \neg P(x)$
2. $\neg(\exists x \, P(x)) \equiv \forall x \, \neg P(x)$

**Intuition:**
- "Not all $x$ satisfy $P$" ↔ "There exists some $x$ that doesn't satisfy $P$"
- "There doesn't exist an $x$ satisfying $P$" ↔ "All $x$ don't satisfy $P$"

### Nested Quantifiers

When predicates involve multiple variables, we use nested quantifiers:

$$\forall x \, \exists y \, P(x, y)$$
means "For every $x$, there exists a $y$ such that $P(x, y)$ is true."

**Order matters!**
$$\forall x \, \exists y \, P(x, y) \not\equiv \exists y \, \forall x \, P(x, y)$$

**Example:**
- $\forall x \, \exists y \, (y > x)$: "For every number $x$, there exists a larger number $y$." (TRUE for real numbers)
- $\exists y \, \forall x \, (y > x)$: "There exists a number $y$ greater than all numbers $x$." (FALSE for real numbers)

## Worked Examples

### Example 1 (Simple): Implication and Its Forms

**Problem:** Given the implication "If it is raining, then the ground is wet," write the converse, inverse, and contrapositive.

**Solution:**

Let $p$ = "It is raining" and $q$ = "The ground is wet."

**Original:** $p \to q$ = "If it is raining, then the ground is wet."

**Converse:** $q \to p$ = "If the ground is wet, then it is raining."

**Inverse:** $\neg p \to \neg q$ = "If it is not raining, then the ground is not wet."

**Contrapositive:** $\neg q \to \neg p$ = "If the ground is not wet, then it is not raining."

**Note:** The contrapositive is logically equivalent to the original, but the converse and inverse are not.

### Example 2 (Intermediate): Evaluating Predicates

**Problem:** Let $P(x)$: "$x^2 < 10$" with domain $\mathbb{Z}$ (integers). Determine the truth value of:

(a) $P(2)$

(b) $P(4)$

(c) $\forall x \, P(x)$

(d) $\exists x \, P(x)$

**Solution:**

(a) $P(2)$: "$2^2 < 10$" → "$4 < 10$" → **True** ✓

(b) $P(4)$: "$4^2 < 10$" → "$16 < 10$" → **False** ✗

(c) $\forall x \, P(x)$: "For all integers $x$, $x^2 < 10$."

Counterexample: $x = 4$ gives $16 \not< 10$. → **False** ✗

(d) $\exists x \, P(x)$: "There exists an integer $x$ such that $x^2 < 10$."

Example: $x = 2$ gives $4 < 10$. → **True** ✓

### Example 3 (Intermediate): Negating Quantified Statements

**Problem:** Negate the following statements and simplify:

(a) $\forall x \, (x > 0)$

(b) $\exists x \, (x^2 = 9)$

(c) $\forall x \, \exists y \, (x + y = 0)$

**Solution:**

**(a)** $\neg(\forall x \, (x > 0)) \equiv \exists x \, \neg(x > 0) \equiv \exists x \, (x \leq 0)$

"Not all $x$ are positive" ↔ "There exists an $x$ that is non-positive."

**(b)** $\neg(\exists x \, (x^2 = 9)) \equiv \forall x \, \neg(x^2 = 9) \equiv \forall x \, (x^2 \neq 9)$

"There doesn't exist an $x$ with $x^2 = 9$" ↔ "For all $x$, $x^2 \neq 9$."

**(c)** $\neg(\forall x \, \exists y \, (x + y = 0)) \equiv \exists x \, \neg(\exists y \, (x + y = 0))$
$$\equiv \exists x \, \forall y \, \neg(x + y = 0)$$
$$\equiv \exists x \, \forall y \, (x + y \neq 0)$$

"Not for all $x$ does there exist a $y$ with $x + y = 0$" ↔ "There exists an $x$ such that for all $y$, $x + y \neq 0$."

### Example 4 (Challenging): Translating Between Natural Language and Logic

**Problem:** Translate the following statements into predicate logic with appropriate quantifiers. Domain: all people.

Let $S(x)$: "$x$ is a student," $L(x, y)$: "$x$ likes $y$"

(a) "Every student likes some professor."

(b) "There is a professor that every student likes."

(c) "No student likes all professors."

**Solution:**

**(a)** "Every student likes some professor."

$$\forall x \, (S(x) \to \exists y \, (\neg S(y) \land L(x, y)))$$

Or with $P(y)$ = "$y$ is a professor":
$$\forall x \, (S(x) \to \exists y \, (P(y) \land L(x, y)))$$

**(b)** "There is a professor that every student likes."

$$\exists y \, (P(y) \land \forall x \, (S(x) \to L(x, y)))$$

**(c)** "No student likes all professors."

$$\neg(\exists x \, (S(x) \land \forall y \, (P(y) \to L(x, y))))$$

Or equivalently:
$$\forall x \, (S(x) \to \exists y \, (P(y) \land \neg L(x, y)))$$

"For every student, there exists a professor they don't like."

---

### Example 5 (Challenging): Proving Logical Equivalence

**Problem:** Prove algebraically: $\neg(p \to q) \equiv p \land \neg q$.

**Solution:**

$$\begin{align}
\neg(p \to q) &\equiv \neg(\neg p \lor q) \quad \text{(implication equivalence)}\\
&\equiv \neg(\neg p) \land \neg q \quad \text{(De Morgan's Law)}\\
&\equiv p \land \neg q \quad \text{(double negation)}
\end{align}$$

Therefore, $\neg(p \to q) \equiv p \land \neg q$. $\square$

**Interpretation:** The negation of "if $p$ then $q$" is "$p$ is true but $q$ is false."

## Applications and Intuition

### Mathematical Proofs
Contrapositive equivalence is invaluable for proofs:
- Direct proof of $p \to q$: assume $p$, derive $q$
- Contrapositive proof: assume $\neg q$, derive $\neg p$

Often, the contrapositive is easier to prove.

### Database Queries
Quantifiers model SQL queries:
- $\forall x \, P(x)$: `SELECT * FROM table WHERE NOT EXISTS (SELECT * FROM table WHERE NOT P(x))`
- $\exists x \, P(x)$: `SELECT * FROM table WHERE P(x)`

### Software Specifications
Predicates specify program behavior:
- Preconditions: $\forall x \, (P(x) \to Q(x))$ — "For all valid inputs $x$, property $Q$ holds"
- Postconditions verify correctness

### Logic Programming
Languages like Prolog use predicates and quantifiers:
```prolog
student(john).
likes(john, math).
```
Query: `?- exists(X, student(X), likes(X, math)).`

### Exercise 5 (Challenge)
Prove using logical equivalences: $(p \land q) \to r \equiv (p \to r) \lor (q \to r)$ is **false**.

**Hint:** Find a counterexample using a truth table or specific truth values.

**Solution:**

Let $p = T$, $q = T$, $r = F$.

**LHS:** $(p \land q) \to r = (T \land T) \to F = T \to F = F$

**RHS:** $(p \to r) \lor (q \to r) = (T \to F) \lor (T \to F) = F \lor F = F$

Both are F for this case. Let's try $p = T$, $q = F$, $r = F$:

**LHS:** $(T \land F) \to F = F \to F = T$

**RHS:** $(T \to F) \lor (F \to F) = F \lor T = T$

Both T. Let's try $p = F$, $q = F$, $r = F$:

**LHS:** $(F \land F) \to F = F \to F = T$

**RHS:** $(F \to F) \lor (F \to F) = T \lor T = T$

Looks like they might be equivalent! Let me check all cases systematically.

Actually, the correct answer is that the statement **is not an equivalence**. The correct equivalence is:
$$(p \land q) \to r \equiv p \to (q \to r)$$

## Common Mistakes and Misconceptions

1. **Confusing converse with contrapositive:** The contrapositive is equivalent to the original, but the converse is not.

2. **Incorrect quantifier negation:** $\neg(\forall x \, P(x)) \equiv \exists x \, \neg P(x)$, NOT $\forall x \, \neg P(x)$.

3. **Ignoring domain:** Always specify the domain when using quantifiers. Truth values depend on it.

4. **Switching quantifier order incorrectly:** $\forall x \, \exists y \, P(x,y)$ is different from $\exists y \, \forall x \, P(x,y)$.

5. **Misusing implication in universal statements:** $\forall x \, (P(x) \to Q(x))$ is correct for "All $P$'s are $Q$'s," not $\forall x \, (P(x) \land Q(x))$.

6. **Negating implications wrong:** $\neg(p \to q) \equiv p \land \neg q$, not $\neg p \to \neg q$.

## Summary and Key Takeaways

1. **Contrapositive equivalence** ($p \to q \equiv \neg q \to \neg p$) is a fundamental proof technique.

2. **Converse and inverse** are not equivalent to the original implication, only to each other.

3. **Predicates** generalize propositions by introducing variables.

4. **Universal quantifier** ($\forall$) asserts truth for all values; **existential quantifier** ($\exists$) asserts truth for at least one value.

5. **Quantifier negation rules** swap $\forall \leftrightarrow \exists$ and negate the predicate.

6. **Nested quantifiers** require careful attention to order, as swapping changes meaning.

7. **Domain specification** is crucial for determining truth values of quantified statements.
